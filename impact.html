Most respondents agree that sargassum negatively affects tourism activities, highlighting widespread concern about its impact on coastal economies. This view is supported by reports explaining that large influxes of sargassum create unsightly and foul-smelling piles along shorelines, which deter tourists and reduce the appeal of beach destinations (Green Caribbean Chronicles, n.d.). In Belize, these conditions can lead to fewer visitors, decreased tourism revenue, and economic strain on communities that depend heavily on tourism-related activities.

Fifty-five percent of respondents believe that sargassum harms marine life, highlighting growing concerns about its impact on coastal ecosystems. This view is supported by reports explaining that excessive sargassum blocks sunlight, which disrupts coral reefs and seagrass beds that depend on light to survive (Green Caribbean Chronicles, n.d.). In Belize, damage to these ecosystems can reduce fish populations, weaken reef protection, and threaten livelihoods that rely on healthy marine environments.

Fifty percent of respondents believe that sargassum causes significant inconvenience to tourism, raising concerns about visitor comfort and beach usability. This view is supported by reports stating that decomposing sargassum emits hydrogen sulfide gas, which can irritate the eyes, throat, and lungs, creating unpleasant and potentially harmful conditions for tourists (Green Caribbean Chronicles, n.d.). In Belize, these conditions may discourage visitors, reduce tourism revenue, and negatively affect communities that rely on clean and accessible beaches.

Sixty percent of respondents believe that sargassum has a major negative effect on tourism and the fishing industry, highlighting serious concerns about its economic impact. This view is supported by reports noting that sargassum disrupts fisheries by tangling fishing gear and making it difficult for boats to navigate through thick mats of seaweed (Blogger, n.d.). In Belize, the 60% support for community-level cleanup shows strong local commitment to protecting beaches. Combined with improved extraction methods, sargassum could be managed more effectively and even reused to benefit coastal communities.
